The Voin Generation: Russia's Militarisation of Children in Russian-occupied Ukraine

Russia is building its next army from the children of Russian-occupied Ukraine — the pipeline, the numbers, and the strategic logic.

THE VOIN GENERATION

Russia's Systematic Militarisation of Children in Occupied Ukrainian Territories and the Coming Strategic Threat

Analytical Paper | Occupied Territories Insight Hub, Centre for Statecraft and National Security (CSNS)

Sources: EHRG/ISRS analytical report (Sep–Oct 2025, 2,641 quantitative data points); Occupied Territories Hub v4 field intelligence (March 2026); Mariupol municipal monitoring; open source procurement and administrative documentation.

Executive Summary

Russia is systematically converting the children of occupied Ukrainian territories into the soldiers of its next war. This is not a side effect of occupation: it is a deliberate, funded, institutionalised programme running at scale across Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhzhia, Kherson, and Crimea oblasts. Between 2014 and 2025, Russia has constructed a comprehensive pipeline — from ideological preschool preparation through military cadet programmes and paramilitary camps to formal Voin ('Warrior') training centres — that is processing an estimated 600,000–650,000 children in the occupied territories.

This paper makes a specific strategic argument: Russia is not merely indoctrinating children to accept occupation. It is manufacturing a cohort of trained, ideologically conditioned military personnel who will be ready for deployment — as conscripts, contract soldiers, or drone operators — within five to seven years. This cohort is designed to resolve Russia's mobilisation problem, which has been acute since 2022, without relying on the Russian metropolitan population whose political disquiet the Kremlin calculates it cannot afford. It is the demographic and military logic of occupation made institutional.

STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT: Children aged 10–16 currently undergoing structured paramilitary and military-technical training in occupied territories will reach conscription age (18) between 2028 and 2034 — precisely the window in which Russia's strategic planners anticipate continued or renewed operations against Ukraine and potential confrontation with NATO's eastern flank.

I. The Scale of the Programme

Headline Numbers

  • 600,000–650,000 total children estimated to have undergone military training in some form in occupied territories
  • 520,000 children recorded in youth militarist organisations as of 2024–2025, up from 20,000 in 2015 — a 26-fold increase
  • ~25,000 minors annually enrolled in ROC-affiliated paramilitary clubs and camps in occupied territories
  • 35,000 children in Yunarmiya (Youth Army) in occupied territories in 2024 alone
  • 80,000 Ukrainian children from occupied territories taken to camps in Crimea and the Russian Federation in summer 2023
  • 1,000,000 children passing through cadet-patriotic education system annually across occupied and annexed territories
  • 45.8 billion rubles directed to summer camps, propaganda events, military-educational programs and youth militarisation tools in 2024
  • Budget for Yunarmiya nearly doubled to almost 1 billion rubles for 2025
  • Voin (Warrior) programme: 31 new representative offices planned to open in 2025; over 1,100 cadets enrolled in occupied regions by start of 2025

The Demographic Arithmetic

The scale of what Russia is constructing becomes clear when considered against the school-age population of occupied territories. As of the 2025 graduating cohort:

  • Occupied Donetsk Oblast: 23,000 total school graduates in 2025 (9,000+ in grade 11, 14,000 in grade 9)
  • Occupied Luhansk Oblast: 17,000+ total graduates (6,000 in grade 11, 11,000+ in grade 9)
  • Occupied Zaporizhzhia Oblast: 7,407 graduates (2,879 in grade 11, 4,528 in grade 9)
  • Occupied Kherson Oblast: 3,843 graduates (1,557 in grade 11, 2,286 in grade 9)

These represent the cohort currently going through or just completing secondary education — and thus completing the militarised curriculum described below. The 2025 grade 9 cohorts (those born approximately 2010–2011) will reach conscription age between 2028 and 2030. The 2025 grade 11 cohorts will reach 18 before this paper's projected window closes. The pipeline is not speculative: it is already passing through.

II. The Architecture of Military Socialisation

Russia has not improvised youth militarisation in occupied Ukraine. It has transplanted and accelerated a system built over fifteen years inside Russia itself, then applied it in occupied territories with characteristic occupation-pace intensity. The system has five interlocking tiers:

Tier 1: Early Indoctrination (Ages 7–10)

Children as young as 7 are eligible for recruitment into military-education clubs. The 'Movement of the Firsts' (successor to Komsomol) operates 190 cells by place of residence in occupied Zaporizhzhia Oblast alone, with 19,000 children enrolled. ROC-affiliated 'Orthodox Knights', 'Guardians of Faith', and 'Patriot' clubs accept children from age 10 and teach basic tactical training, shooting, weapons handling, and the ideology of 'serving Russia'. At least 150 such clubs and camps operate in occupied territories under ROC auspices.

In Mariupol, field intelligence from March 2026 documents the construction of the 'Patriot' park and 'Voin' military-sports training and patriotic education centre on the site of the former Ukrainian children's camp 'Orlyonok'. Planned facilities include: a tactical training house, UAV training area, shooting gallery, stadium, sports halls, a tactical and field medicine training block, and a training helicopter landing site — designed to accommodate up to 300 children. This is the physical replacement of Ukrainian civic heritage with Russian military infrastructure.

Tier 2: Military Curriculum in Schools (Grades 5–11, Ages 11–17)

From 2022, a new mandatory subject — 'Fundamentals of Homeland Security and Defence' (OBZH/Bezopasnost Rodiny) — was introduced for grades 8–11 across all schools in occupied territories. In grades 8 and 10, this includes mandatory practical training sessions at military training grounds simulating army conditions. Several times per year, all students in these grades participate in field exercises.

Cadet classes — specialist military-track classes — have been established in:

  • 86 cadet classes in occupied Luhansk Oblast (as of 2023–2024)
  • 62 Cossack cadet classes in occupied Luhansk Oblast
  • 260 cadet classes in occupied Crimea (representing nearly tenfold growth)
  • 13 cadet classes in occupied Zaporizhzhia Oblast
  • 9 cadet classes in occupied Kherson Oblast

Children from Donbas are enrolled in the departmental cadet corps of the Investigative Committee of the Russian Federation. In March 2026, field intelligence from the Occupied Territories Hub documented school children at Mariupol School No. 28 undergoing cadet initiation ceremonies alongside students from Volgograd — explicitly integrating them into 'an all-Russian cadet brotherhood, replacing Ukrainian cultural and civic identity with Russian institutional belonging.' At School No. 53, Mariupol, the Investigative Committee conducted an interactive game in which children played roles as investigators, prosecutors, and 'corrupt officials' — described in official reporting as 'a professional starting point for future Investigative Committee officers.'

The psychological dimension is also being institutionalised: a new teenage psychological centre opened in Mariupol in March 2026, with official messaging framing it as developing 'emotional intelligence, creativity, and responsibility' — but, as the field intelligence notes, in an occupation context this represents 'institutional incorporation of teenagers into a system of controlled psychological and pedagogical supervision: a mechanism for shaping loyalty, correcting behaviour, and imposing approved social adaptation models.'

Tier 3: The Voin (Warrior) Centres

The Voin programme is the centrepiece of Russia's youth military training infrastructure in occupied territories. Voin centres provide structured paramilitary training for children aged 14–17, with extended programmes for students up to 18 and reservists up to 35. As of early 2025:

  • 15,000 young people had started classes at Voin centres across Russia, with 35,000 the planned annual target
  • 1,100 cadets from the 'new regions' (occupied territories) enrolled as of early 2025
  • 500+ people processed through the Luhansk Voin centre; 300 in training simultaneously; 800–1,000 projected by spring 2025
  • 300 teenagers recruited to the Kherson Voin centre in its first batch (April 2024, ages 14–17)
  • More than 85% of Voin instructors are military personnel who have taken part in armed aggression against Ukraine
  • A full training course on working with UAS (unmanned aerial systems) at Voin centres runs 256 hours, including a dedicated 'UAS Operator' module
  • Training covers 8 key military-applied disciplines: tactics, shooting, first aid, engineering, navigation, UAV operation, communications, and physical conditioning

The Voin programme is not simply patriotic education. It is structured pre-military training aligned with the specific capability gaps Russia faces in its current war: drone operation, field medicine, navigation, and tactical awareness.

Tier 4: Paramilitary Summer Camps and Inter-regional Integration

To deepen the integration of occupied-territory youth into Russian military socialisation networks, children are transported to paramilitary camps across Russia:

  • 2,500 teenagers from Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhzhia, and Kherson oblasts underwent training at the Avangard camp in Volgograd Oblast in summer 2024 — three-week programmes
  • 140 teenagers from Donetsk sent to Volgograd for one summer shift
  • 59 Kherson teenagers sent to camps in other regions; 44 from Zaporizhzhia
  • 7 Youth Army summer camp shifts conducted in Mariupol in summer 2024
  • Youth Army membership in occupied Crimea: 1,500 children in 2017; 15,000 by November 2024 — a tenfold increase

This inter-regional transportation serves a dual function: it provides more intensive training than is available locally, and it creates physical and psychological ties between occupied-territory children and Russian institutional life — making return to Ukraine identity more difficult.

Tier 5: Drone Training and Military-Technical Preparation

Perhaps the most significant long-term dimension of Russia's youth militarisation in occupied territories is the systematic introduction of drone and unmanned aerial systems (UAS) training to teenagers. This addresses Russia's most acute tactical manpower problem: trained drone operators at scale.

  • At least 5 training grounds and schools for UAS operator training established in occupied territories as of February 2025
  • At least 8 industrial enterprises in occupied territories involved in UAS component production and assembly
  • 10,000 teenagers involved in practical drone pilot training programmes in occupied territories
  • 3 schools in Mariupol where Young Guard-Yunarmiya members are trained to control drones
  • UAV research laboratory opened at Priazovsky State Technical University (Mariupol) in 2024
  • Student groups of 25 formed for UAV operator training in Luhansk, with top students offered free further education
  • Drone training introduced for disabled children in occupied Kherson Oblast, twice weekly from April 2024
  • Children aged 8–12 competing in drone racing competitions in occupied Kherson Oblast (May 2024)

The scale of Russia's drone training infrastructure nationally contextualises the occupied-territory programmes. Russia plans to train 1,000,000 UAS specialists by 2030, having introduced drone training to 523 schools nationally with 30 practical training centres. The Voin centres' 256-hour UAS course is the occupied-territory manifestation of this national programme. Experienced gamers require only 1–2 days additional training to become operational FPV drone operators — a fact that Russian military planners have explicitly noted.

III. Historical Precedent: Making Soldiers of Children

The system Russia has constructed in occupied Ukraine is unprecedented in post-Cold War Europe but has direct historical antecedents. Three comparisons illuminate the structural logic and the likely operational trajectory.

Hitlerjugend (1933–1945)

The most directly analogous precedent is the compulsory militarisation of German youth under National Socialism. Between 1933 and 1939, the Hitler Youth grew from 100,000 to 8.8 million members, eventually becoming compulsory in 1939. The programme combined political indoctrination, physical conditioning, weapons familiarisation, and ideological commitment to the German state. By 1944–1945, multiple divisions of the Wehrmacht were composed primarily of Hitlerjugend-trained teenagers — including the 12th SS Panzer Division 'Hitlerjugend', deployed against Allied forces in Normandy.

The parallel with Russia's occupied-territory programme is structural: compulsory integration into a military-political youth organisation, physical and weapons training from early adolescence, ideological displacement of prior identity (Ukrainian, in this case), and an explicit pipeline from youth organisation to armed service. The Russian programme's timeline — 5 to 10 years before the 'Voin generation' replaces the current adult cohort in recruitment pools — mirrors the accelerated militarisation timeline that made Hitlerjugend into fighting forces.

Soviet Pioneers and DOSAAF

The Soviet system of youth militarisation — Pioneers (from age 10), Komsomol (from 14), and the DOSAAF voluntary defence society — provides a closer institutional model. DOSAAF's explicit function was pre-military preparation: teaching young men to shoot, parachute, operate radio equipment, and drive military vehicles. Ukraine's eastern oblasts were deeply integrated into this system before 1991, and many of the adults now administering Russia's occupation education system were themselves trained through it.

Russia is not reinventing the wheel. It is reinstalling one that was removed after 1991. The cossack cadet classes, the OBZH military subject, the Voin centres, and the Yunarmiya are institutional descendants of a system that was highly effective at producing military-ready conscripts — with updated content (FPV drones replacing radio operation) but the same structural purpose.

The Khmer Rouge and Child Soldiers (1975–1979)

A darker parallel — geographically distant but structurally relevant — is the Khmer Rouge's separation of children from their families and integration into military and political units. Unlike the German and Soviet parallels, this comparison highlights the separation mechanism that Russia is deploying: the transportation of thousands of children to camps inside Russia itself, away from family and Ukrainian social networks, for extended periods of ideological and paramilitary conditioning. The parallel is not in the extremity of violence but in the deliberate use of physical separation to accelerate identity transformation.

IV. Strategic Implications for European Security

The Mobilisation Problem and the Occupied-Territory Solution

Russia's current war in Ukraine has exposed a structural weakness in Russian military mobilisation: the political cost of conscripting men from Russian metropolitan areas (Moscow, St. Petersburg) is substantially higher than the Kremlin calculates it can manage. Mobilisation protests in September 2022 were contained but alarming. The systematic resort to recruiting migrants from Turkmenistan and Uzbekistan — documented in Mariupol in March 2026, with 84 migrants mobilised in a single raid under guise of 'migration inspections' — reflects the acute shortage of willing domestic recruits.

Occupied-territory populations resolve this problem from Russia's perspective. They are:

  • Demographically captive: unable to leave without Russian documentation and subject to forced conscription.
  • Legally Russian citizens: subject to Russian conscription law after naturalisation, which is being accelerated (Presidential Decree No. 140, March 4, 2026 simplified citizenship acquisition for occupied territories).
  • Ideologically prepared: children processed through the Voin programme and cadet system will have internalised Russian military identity before reaching conscription age.
  • Geographically isolated: far from the protest networks and media environment that constrain mobilisation in Russian cities.

Field intelligence from the Occupied Territories Hub (March 2026) documents the complete rejection of military contract recruitment by all detainees in Mariupol's pre-trial detention centre in early March — the military commissariat subsequently ceased treating the SIZO as a recruitment source. This is evidence not of resistance success but of the occupier's problem: adult occupied-territory populations retain Ukrainian identity and refuse military service in large numbers. The Voin generation is designed to replace them.

The 5–7 Year Operational Window

Children aged 10–14 currently undergoing structured Voin training will reach conscription age between 2030 and 2034. Children aged 14–17 currently in Voin centres will reach 18 between 2027 and 2030. The first cohort of children who received essentially their entire secondary education under the Russian occupation curriculum — entering school in 2022 at age 6–7, completing grade 11 in 2033–2034 at age 17–18 — will reach full military maturity in the mid-2030s.

PROJECTION: By 2030, Russia will have processed multiple annual cohorts of 10,000–20,000 trained, ideologically conditioned military-capable young people from occupied Ukrainian territories. These cohorts represent a locally recruited, legally conscriptable force that does not require the domestic political management that metropolitan Russian conscription demands. This is a structural change to Russia's mobilisation capacity, not a marginal addition to it.

The Drone Operator Dimension

The specific emphasis on drone and UAS training in occupied territories is not incidental. It addresses Russia's most acute near-term tactical shortfall: the training of FPV drone operators at scale is a bottleneck in Russian military effectiveness. The 256-hour Voin UAS curriculum, the drone training introduced in Mariupol schools, and the opening of the UAV research laboratory at Priazovsky Technical University together represent a systematic effort to build drone operator capacity in the population closest to the front. Children who learn to fly FPV drones at ages 12–15 are, by military estimation, operational after 1–2 additional days of tactical training.

Implications for European Defence Planning

The Voin generation represents a category of threat that European defence planners have not systematically addressed: a locally sourced, ideologically prepared military cohort manufactured inside the borders of a European state by an occupying power. Three implications warrant attention:

1. Counter-drone and electronic warfare capacity

If Russia successfully builds a cadre of tens of thousands of drone-trained young people from occupied territories, the density and sophistication of drone warfare against Ukrainian and NATO positions will increase qualitatively in the 2028–2035 window. European investment in counter-drone capabilities needs to be calibrated to this scenario.

2. The accountability and recovery dimension

Children who have been systematically militarised through this programme are simultaneously victims of the occupation and potential security challenges during any future reintegration. Ukraine and its partners will face a substantial challenge: how to deradicalise and reintegrate a generation that has been through 5–10 years of systematic Russian military-political conditioning. This requires early-stage planning, now.

3. Documentation as strategic intelligence

The names of commanders of Voin centres, teachers implementing the OBZH curriculum, officials who approved the conversion of Orlyonok camp to a military training facility — these are documented in the Occupied Territories Hub and the EHRG named-individual database. They are evidence for accountability and also intelligence for understanding the institutional architecture of the programme. Sustained investment in this documentation capacity serves both legal and strategic purposes.

V. Conclusions

Russia is constructing a generation of soldiers in occupied Ukrainian territory. The evidence is quantitative (520,000 children in militarist organisations, 600,000–650,000 processed through military training, 45.8 billion rubles budgeted for youth militarisation in 2024), institutional (Voin centres, cadet classes, OBZH curriculum, ROC paramilitary clubs), and spatial (construction of a military training complex on the former Orlyonok children's camp in Mariupol, drone training in schools, UAV laboratory at Mariupol's technical university).

The strategic logic is coherent and visible: Russia needs military manpower it can conscript without incurring the political costs of metropolitan mobilisation. Occupied-territory children, processed through a decade-long pipeline of ideological conditioning and military training, are designed to provide it. The 5–7 year horizon for their operational readiness corresponds to the strategic timeline Russian planners are working to.

Two historical comparisons are instructive but also cautionary: Germany's Hitlerjugend was effective until it wasn't, and the human cost of its effectiveness was catastrophic. The Soviet DOSAAF produced effective soldiers who subsequently had to be demobilised into peaceful lives when the political context changed. In both cases, the programme was ultimately reversible — but reversal required either military defeat or political transformation. The Voin generation is a strategic problem with a strategic solution requirement.

For policy, the immediate implication is straightforward: the Occupied Territories Insight Hub's documentation of this programme — individual-level, institution-level, and programme-level — is strategic intelligence as much as it is evidence for accountability. Maintaining and expanding that documentation capacity, and ensuring it reaches both legal and defence-planning audiences, is the highest-priority contribution research can make to addressing this threat.
